Clustering With Elbow Method Optimization¶

In this section, we will use the elbow method to determine the optimal number of clusters for K-means clustering based on longitude, latitude, and median income values. Rather than only clustering on geographic location, we want to identify clusters that consider both spatial and socioeconomic factors. We will plot the elbow plot to visualize the inertia (within-cluster sum of squares) against different numbers of clusters, revealing the point where the inertia starts to decrease at a slower rate, indicating the optimal cluster count. Then, we perform K-means clustering with the optimal number of clusters and visualize the resulting clusters on a scatter plot.

In [10]:
X = df[['longitude', 'latitude', 'median_income']]

# Create Elbow Plot To Find Optimal Number Of Clusters
inertia = []
k_range = range(1, 11)
for i in k_range:
    kmeans = KMeans(n_clusters=i, random_state=42, n_init = 10)
    kmeans.fit(X)
    inertia.append(kmeans.inertia_)

# Plot 
plt.plot(k_range, inertia, marker='o')
plt.title('Elbow Plot for Clustering')
plt.xlabel('Number of Clusters')
plt.ylabel('Inertia')
plt.xticks(k_range)
plt.show()

Analyzing this elbow plot, we see that the major declines in slope are at n=2 and n=3 clusters. We will focus on using 3 clusters because it strikes a balance between simplicity and an overly complex segmentation. Additionally, this will allow us to gain a meaningful interpretation on the housing and socioeconomic patterns of these different groups.

This process essentially divides the data into groups based on similarities in the given features.

Conclusion¶

Key Findings:¶

  1. The K-means clustering analysis identified three distinct clusters within the California housing market based on geographical coordinates and median income.

  2. Cluster one emerged as the most 'affluent', characterized by the highest median income and housing value, predominantly located in coastal areas with newer properties. This was corroborated by boxplot analysis, which revealed cluster one's dominance in both a higher income and a lower housing age.

  3. Furthermore, a positive correlation between median income and house value was evident from the scatter plot, emphasizing the significance of socioeconomic factors in driving housing prices. Additionally, this suggests that the cluster with the highest median income (one) would also have the highest housing value.

  4. Additionally, the stacked bar plot showcased the prevalence of coastal proximity in cluster one, underscoring the allure and premium associated with oceanfront living.

  5. Together, these insights provide a comprehensive understanding of the spatial and socioeconomic dynamics shaping the California housing market.
